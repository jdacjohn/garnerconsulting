June, 2008

PRESIDENT SIGNS HEART ACT

On June 17, 2008, the President signed H.R. 6081, the Heroes Earnings Assistance and Relief Tax Act of 2008. Among other things, this bill retroactively reinstates the Mental Health Parity Act of 1996, which had expired on December 31, 2007. Both houses of Congress have passed bills that would expand the requirements of the Mental Health Parity Act, but a conference committee has yet to resolve differences between the two versions. Published reports indicate that conferees have agreed to drop a provision in the House version that would have required any plan that covers mental health benefits to cover any diagnosis in the American Psychiatric Association’s Diagnostic and Statistical Manual, which includes such things as caffeine addiction and jet lag.

As reported in last month’s bulletin, H.R. 6081 also allows employers to amend their flexible spending accounts to permit reservists called up for at least 6 months of active duty to take FSA balances as a taxable distribution in cash. The new law does not address how this distribution right impacts the employer's coverage continuation obligations under both USERRA and COBRA. In addition, the bill does not amend Code Section 105, which disqualifies plans that allow reimbursements for other than medical care expenses.

CALIFORNIA ASSEMBLY APPROVES BILL TO REQUIRE SICK LEAVE

On May 28, 2008, the California Assembly approved A.B. 2716, which would require employers to provide paid sick days.

Under the bill, employees would be entitled to use accrued sick days beginning on the 90th calendar day of employment. Employees would accrue sick days at the rate of one hour for every 30 hours worked.

The bill would require employers to provide paid sick days, upon the request of the employee, for diagnosis, care or treatment of health conditions of the employee or an employee’s family member or for leave related to domestic violence or sexual assault. An employer would be prohibited from discriminating or retaliating against an employee who requests paid sick days.

The bill would also require employers to satisfy specified posting, notice and recordkeeping requirements.

SUPREME COURT RULES ON DISCRETIONARY CLAUSES

The Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA) permits a person denied benefits under an employee benefit plan to challenge that denial in federal court. Often the entity that administers the plan, such as an employer or an insurance company, both determines whether an employee is eligible for benefits and pays benefits out of its own pocket. The United States Supreme Court has decided that this dual role creates a conflict of interest; and that a reviewing court should consider that conflict as a factor in determining whether the plan administrator has abused its discretion in denying benefits; and that the significance of the factor will depend upon the circumstances of the particular case.

This case is important, because under its Firestone ruling of almost two decades ago, the Supreme Court said that if an ERISA plan grants discretion to someone to interpret the terms of the plan, a court will only overturn that decision if the decision was arbitrary and capricious. If the ERISA documents do not grant discretion, then courts will look at the case without regard to the plan’s decision. Now the court has created a middle ground in which a court can decide just how much deference to give to the decision of a person (or entity) with discretion who also has a conflict of interest.

Metropolitan Life Insurance Company (MetLife) serves as both an administrator and the insurer of Sears, Roebuck & Company’s long-term disability insurance plan, an ERISA-governed employee benefit plan. The plan grants MetLife (as administrator) discretionary authority to determine whether an employee’s claim for benefits is valid; it simultaneously provides that MetLife (as insurer) will itself pay valid benefit claims.

Wanda Glenn, a Sears employee, applied for disability benefits and MetLife concluded that she met the plan’s standard for an initial 24 months of benefits. MetLife also directed Glenn to a law firm that would assist her in applying for Social Security disability benefits. An Administrative Law Judge found that Glenn’s illness prevented her not only from performing her own job but also “from performing any jobs [for which she could qualify] existing in significant numbers in the national economy.” The Social Security Administration consequently granted Glenn permanent disability payments retroactively.

To continue receiving Sears plan disability benefits after 24 months, Glenn had to meet a stricter, Social-Security-type standard, namely, that her medical condition rendered her incapable of performing not only her own job but of performing “the material duties of any gainful occupation for which” she was “reasonably qualified.” MetLife denied Glenn this extended benefit because it found that she was “capable of performing full time sedentary work.”

After exhausting her administrative remedies, Glenn brought this federal lawsuit, seeking judicial review of MetLife’s denial of benefits. The District Court denied relief. Glenn appealed to the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit. The Court of Appeals treated “as a relevant factor” a “conflict of interest” arising out of the fact that MetLife was “authorized both to decide whether an employee is eligible for benefits and to pay those benefits.”

The Court of Appeals set aside MetLife’s denial of benefits and MetLife asked the Supreme Court to determine whether a plan administrator that both evaluates and pays claims operates under a conflict of interest in making discretionary benefit determinations. The Supreme Court concluded that it does.

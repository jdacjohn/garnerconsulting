July, 2008

HOUSE PASSES BILL EXPANDING COVERAGE FOR ILL STUDENTS

On July 30, 2008, the US House of Representatives passed H.R. 2851, affecting when coverage for a dependent child can be terminated under the parent’s medical plan.

Known as Michelle’s Law, medical plans will be prohibited from terminating coverage of a dependent child due to a medically necessary leave of absence from a postsecondary education institution or any other change in enrollment at that institution. “Medically Necessary” is defined as a severe illness or injury that causes the child to lose full-time student status before the earlier of one year after the first day of the leave or the date on which coverage would otherwise terminate under the plan. A severe illness or injury requires certification by the child’s physician who will also determine the date the leave begins.

Michelle’s Law amends The Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974, the Public Health Service Act and the Internal Revenue Code and will apply to group (whether insured or self-funded) as well as individual medical plans. The effective date would be for medically necessary leaves beginning in plan years one year after the date the President signs the Legislation. There’s no effective date exception for Collective Bargaining Agreements in the current legislation.

Children are generally dropped from their parent’s health insurance plan’s at age 18 or 19 or at age 24 or 25 if full time students at an accredited college. New Jersey requires coverage for children up to age 30 as long as they have no dependents of their own. At least four States also recognize grandchildren as dependents. It’s evident that coverage for dependent children is rapidly expanding at the state and federal level. It’s too early to assess the cost impact of Michelle’s Law.

CONGRESS OVERRIDES VETO OF MEDICARE BILL

On July 15, 2008, the US Congress overrode the President’s veto of the Medicare Improvement for Patients and Providers Act of 2008, which makes a number of changes to Medicare, including

  • Changing the rules regarding coverage of preventive services. Previously, it literally took an act of Congress to cover a preventive service. Now, the Secretary of Health and Human Services can decide which preventive services will be covered under Medicare;
  • Gradually reducing copayments for psychiatric services;
  • Increasing physicians’ payments;
  • Providing incentives for electronic prescriptions;
  • Providing coverage for cardiac and pulmonary rehabilitation;
  • Revising requirements for Medicare Advantage private fee-for-service plans.

    • IRS ISSUES GUIDANCE RELATED TO HDHPs AND HSAs

    On June 3, 2008, the IRS released Notice 2008-52 providing guidance on the tax implications for people who only have a high deductible health plan (HDHP) for part of a year. There is a “testing period” that begins on the first day of the last month of the taxable year and ends on the last day of the 12th month following that month. For a calendar year taxpayer, the testing period begins on December 1 and ends on December 31 of the next year.

    If an individual who is eligible to establish an Health Savings Account (HSA) on the first day of the testing period ceases to be eligible later in the testing period (other than due to disability or death), contributions to the HSA in excess of the sum of the monthly contribution limits will be included in income and subject to an additional 10% tax.

    On July 21, 2008, the IRS released Notice 2008-59 providing guidance on a number of issues related to HSAs. Among other things, the guidance indicates that if an individual switches from a family HDHP to a self-only HDHP, the self-only HDHP may use any reasonable method to allocate the covered expenses incurred during the period of family coverage for the purpose of satisfying the deductible for self-only coverage.

    2009 LIMITS FOR HEALTH SAVINGS ACCOUNTS

    On June 2, 2008, the IRS announced the following cost-of-living adjustments for 2009 related to HSAs:

    Maximum Annual Contribution (Self) $3,000 (up from $2,900 in 2008)
    Maximum Annual Contribution (Family) $5,950 (up from $5,800 in 2008)
    Minimum Annual Deductible (Self) $1,150 (up from $1,100 in 2008)
    Maximum Annual Contribution (Family) $2,300 (up from $2,200 in 2008)
    Minimum Annual Deductible (Self) $5,800 (up from $5,600 in 2008)
    Maximum Annual Contribution (Family) $11,600 (up from $11,200 in 2008)

    By statute, the catch-up contribution will increase to $1,000 for people age 55 and over in 2009.

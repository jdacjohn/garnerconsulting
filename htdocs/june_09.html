IRS POSTS MORE Q&As ABOUT COBRA PREMIUM SUBSIDY

The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009 (ARRA, commonly known as the "Economic Stimulus Package") created a COBRA premium subsidy whereby qualified beneficiaries who are involuntarily terminated between September 1, 2008 and December 31, 2009 (known as "Assistance Eligible Individuals" or AEIs) need to pay only 35% of the normal COBRA premium for a limited period of time. The employer pays the other 65% of the COBRA premium and is reimbursed for these costs in the form of a payroll tax credit.

The IRS has updated the Q&As posted on its web site under the heading "COBRA: Answers for Employers."

Answers to new questions under "Reporting and documentation" discuss the documentation needed by an insurer or multi-employer plan to establish that an employee is eligible for the premium assistance subsidy by reason of an involuntary termination of employment, confirm that the amount of the premium assistance subsidy need not be reported on an employees Form W-2 or Form 1099, and address who can claim the payroll tax credit in various situations involving employees of 2 or more unrelated employers, situations involving employers in a controlled group and situations involving employers who are agencies of a state government.

The answer to a new question under "Form preparation" advises that if an agent is filing returns and making deposits and payments on behalf of an employer under a Form 2678, the employer would claim the payroll tax credit on any payroll tax return filed by the agent, and the agent should reduce any payroll tax deposits and payments made on behalf of the employer by the amount of the credit to which the employer is entitled.

Answers to new questions under "Administration and eligibility" provide additional guidance on what constitutes an "involuntary termination of employment" under ARRA's COBRA provisions. Of interest is that the IRS will not challenge an employer's "reasonable interpretation" that is consistent with the applicable COBRA provisions in determining that employer is entitled to a payroll tax credit based upon the premium assistance afforded to a former employee.

Another answer notes that the premium assistance subsidy is not available under voluntary continuation coverage that an employer may provide if the employer is not subject to any COBRA continuation coverage requirements as required by ARRA. So, for example, a church plan that is exempt from federal COBRA requirements and is not covered under any comparable state law requirements would not be able to provide any premium assistance subsidy for former employees.

Other new Q&As extend the definition of an "involuntary termination" of employment to seasonal workers hired for a limited period of time or to teachers hired under a one-year contract that is non-renewed, elected officials who are not re-elected or who are "termed out" under applicable term limitation requirements (but not elected officials who choose not to run for re-election), and members of a military Reserve Unit or the National Guard who are called to active duty. Other Q&As address eligibility for premium assistance under multiemployer plans in which employees may have "banked" hours prior to their involuntary termination of employment and in the case of an assistance-eligible individual eligible for or covered by Tricare, the length of time an assistance eligible individual may receive the premium assistance subsidy if he or she elects COBRA to be effective later than the first coverage period beginning on or after February 17, 2009, and clarify that COBRA premiums paid on a pretax basis from an individual's HRA are considered to be paid by the employer and hence not eligible for the premium assistance subsidy.

The updated Q&As may be viewed at http://www.irs.gov/newsroom/article/0,,id=204708,00.html.

HEALTH CARE REFORM PROGRESSING, BUT PACE MAY BE SLOWING

Congress has held numerous hearings on health care reform, but a few key issues have emerged that have the potential to derail the process. One of the key issues is whether or not health care reform will include a public plan option. Advocates argue that it would provide more choice to consumers and keep costs affordable. Many doctors and hospitals are opposed to a public plan because they fear they would be underpaid, as they are now when they treat Medicare and Medicaid patients. Insurers are opposed to a public plan because they fear they could not possibly compete with a plan that could arbitrarily set payments to providers at artificially low levels. Opponents of a single-payer system are against a public plan because they fear that it would lead to a collapse of the private insurance market, leaving the public plan as a de facto single-payer plan. Efforts to reach a compromise on this issue have focused on either a weak public plan that would not set provider reimbursement rates as low as Medicare does or having the public plan as a fallback if the private insurance system fails to offer enough consumer choices at affordable levels.

Other stumbling blocks to reform include the high cost of reform and how to pay for it and whether reform should include a requirement that all employers provide health insurance to their employees.

2010 LIMITS FOR HEALTH SAVINGS ACCOUNTS

On June 1, 2009, the IRS announced the following inflation adjustments for 2010 related to HSAs:

Maximum Annual Contribution

  • Self $3,050 (up from $3,000 in 2009)
  • Family $6,150 (up from $5,950 in 2009)

Minimum Annual Deductible for High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)

  • Self $1,200 (up from $1,150 in 2009)
  • Family $2,400 (up from $2,300 in 2009)

Maximum Out-of-Pocket Limit for HDHP

  • Self $5,950 (up from $5,800 in 2009)
  • Family $11,900 (up from $11,600 in 2009)

By statute, the catch-up contribution will remain $1,000 for people age 55 and over. HDHPs and HSAs provide valuable tools for both employers and employees to mitigate the ever increasing cost of health insurance. Please contact us for more information.

HHS & FTC ISSUE FINAL ARRA SECURITY BREACH RULES

On August 24 the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) issued an interim final rule to implement the security breach notification requirements required by The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009 (ARRA, commonly known as the "Stimulus Package"). On August 25 the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) followed by issuing its final rule on this subject. The HHS interim final rule under HIPAA's administrative simplification provisions will affect covered entities and their business associates and will be effective September 23, 2009. Due to concerns expressed during the public comment period and ambiguities in the statute, HHS will not impose sanctions for failure to provide the required notifications for breaches that are discovered before February 22, 2010. The FTC's final rule will affect other entities not covered by HIPAA are effective September 24, 2009, but again, full compliance with the FTC regulations will not be required until February 22, 2010.

In this interim final rule, HHS has updated its guidance regarding when notification of a breach of unsecured protected health information (PHI) is required under its and the FTC's rules. Encryption and destruction remain the sole technologies and methodologies that satisfy the safe harbor allowing covered entities to avoid the notification requirements in the event of a security breach under this interim final rule. This updated guidance specifically notes that only destruction of paper PHI satisfies the safe harbor requirements; mere redaction of paper-based PHI is not sufficient. HHS also noted in the preamble that this updated guidance does not modify a covered entity's responsibilities under the HIPAA Security Rule nor does it require a covered entity to encrypt all PHI. HHS also noted that a covered entity may be in compliance with the Security Rule even if it reasonably decides not to encrypt PHI. However, should a covered entity choose to encrypt PHI, the guidance notes that covered entities and business associates should maintain encryption keys on a device separate from the data they encrypt or decrypt.

HHS also clarified the definition of breach of PHI which would require notification to the individual or other parties. Under this clarified definition, a covered entity will need to perform a risk assessment to determine if there is a significant risk of harm to the individual as a result of the impermissible use or disclosure. If the nature of the PHI used or disclosed does not pose a significant risk of financial, reputational or other harm, then the violation is not a breach. Accordingly the risk assessment must be fact specific. The rule also provides additional details regarding the timing, content and method of notification to individuals, the media and HHS. HHS will post additional instructions on its web site regarding the annual notification of breaches involving less than 500 individuals and the notification to HHS for breaches involving 500 or more individuals.

The FTC's final rule makes very few changes to the proposed rule published in April 2009. The changes result from the FTC's attempt to make its requirements consistent with HHS's interim final rule. Under this final rule the FTC has made sure that its regulations, which apply to vendors of personal health records and their third-party service providers and other related entities do not overlap with HHS's requirements in order to avoid consumers receiving multiple notifications of the same breach of electronic PHI.

HHS and the FTC are requesting public comment on these rules. The HHS interim final rule is at http://edocket.access.gpo.gov/2009/pdf/E9-20169.pdf. The FTC final rule is at http://edocket.access.gpo.gov/2009/pdf/E9-20142.pdf.

IRS ALLOWS DEPOSITING UNUSED VACATION AND SICK LEAVE TO 401(k)s

In his weekly radio address on September 5, 2009, the President proposed a number of new initiatives to help families save for retirement. One of them is to allow unused leave to be converted to 401(k) savings. Accordingly, the IRS released Revenue Rulings 2009-31 and 2009-32 to clarify steps that employers can take immediately to contribute unused vacation and sick leave to 401(k) accounts. These rulings describe how employers can permit employees to convert the value of their unused leave into additional contributions to tax-qualified Section 401(k), profit sharing or stock bonus plans. Revenue Ruling 2009-31 has to do with amounts of leave that would be forfeited at year-end. Revenue Ruling 2009-32 has to do with unused leave at the time of termination.

Employers are not required to make any changes, but the rulings give examples of the kinds of changes employers could make to their plans, if they choose to do so.

The rulings say that because the amounts contributed will vary based on the amount of the participant's unused paid time off, the contributions are likely to preclude a plan from satisfying a design-based safe harbor. Therefore, testing based on the contributions made for individual participants generally will be required. Furthermore, contributions made pursuant to these arrangements must not exceed the Section 415 limits.

The rulings can be found at http://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rr-09-31.pdf and http://www.irs.gov/pub/irsdrop/rr-09-32.pdf .

HEALTH CARE REFORM UPDATE

During the week of September 21, 2009, the Senate Finance Committee began to mark up (the process by which congressional committees and subcommittees debate, amend, and rewrite proposed legislation) health care reform legislation that many observers believe will become the basis for the final version of health care reform, if Congress is able to enact health care reform this year. Mark up of the bill will continue during the week of September 28, 2009 and possibly beyond.

The bill in the Senate Finance Committee, written by chairman Max Baucus (D-MT) has yet to gather any support from Republicans. Now that the late Senator Kennedy (D-MA) has been replaced, Democrats and their independent allies in the Senate control 60 votes, so Republican support would not be needed to pass a bill, if Senator Byrd (D-WV) is healthy enough to vote. Even though the Democrats may have the votes needed to pass a bill, the President has repeatedly stated his preference for a bipartisan measure.

The Senate Finance Committee modified the bill submitted by Senator Baucus in a number of ways during the first week of debate, dealing with over 60 amendments (more than 500 remain to be addressed). Only four amendments proposed by Republicans were approved. One amendment that passed added more subsidies to help lower-income people buy insurance. Another amendment that was approved reduced proposed taxes on insurance plans with high premiums.
